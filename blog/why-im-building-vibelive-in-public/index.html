Why I'm Building VibeLive in Public

behind-the-scenesvibelive

There’s something terrifying about building in public. Every misstep is visible. Every pivot is documented. Every “I have no idea what I’m doing” moment is out there for the world to see.

So why do it?

The honest answer

Because building alone is slow. Not just technically — mentally. When you’re working on something as ambitious as a real-time video SDK, you need more than just code reviews. You need people who care about the outcome.

Building in public creates that. It turns spectators into stakeholders.

What “in public” actually means for us

I’m not talking about tweeting every commit. Here’s what building in public looks like for VibeLive:

  • Sharing the reasoning behind decisions, not just the decisions themselves
  • Being transparent about trade-offs — what we chose, what we gave up, and why
  • Showing real progress, including the weeks where progress means “we rewrote the same thing three times”
  • Engaging with developers who are building with real-time video

The selfish reason

Feedback loops. The tighter they are, the better the product gets.

When I share what I’m working on, developers respond with their actual pain points. Not hypothetical ones I imagined at my desk — real ones, from real projects. That’s invaluable.

What I’ve learned so far

Three things stand out:

  1. People respect honesty more than polish. A rough demo with a genuine explanation outperforms a polished landing page every time.
  2. The community finds bugs you never would. Fresh eyes on your architecture catch assumptions you’ve been blind to for months.
  3. Momentum is contagious. When people see consistent progress, they want to be part of it.

What’s next

I’ll be sharing more about the technical decisions behind VibeLive — how we handle latency, why we chose certain protocols, and what makes building a video SDK different from building with one.

If you’re working on anything with real-time video, I’d love to hear what’s frustrating you. The best features come from real problems.
