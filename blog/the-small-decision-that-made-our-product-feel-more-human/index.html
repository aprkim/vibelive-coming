The Small Decision That Made Our Product Feel More Human

productdeveloper-experience

A few weeks ago, I changed one small thing in VibeLive. It took maybe thirty minutes to implement. But it fundamentally changed how the SDK feels to use.

Here’s what happened.

The problem no one complained about

When a participant joins a video session through VibeLive, there’s a brief moment — usually 200-400ms — where the connection is being established. During that time, the video element is just… empty. A black rectangle.

No one filed a bug about it. No one mentioned it in feedback. It’s technically correct behavior. The stream hasn’t started yet, so there’s nothing to show.

But every time I saw it during testing, something felt off.

What we tried

The obvious solution was a loading spinner. We tried it. It worked, technically. But it made the experience feel slower. A spinner says “wait” — it draws attention to the delay rather than smoothing over it.

Then we tried a skeleton placeholder with a subtle pulse animation. Better, but it felt generic. Like every other loading state on the internet.

What we actually did

We added a soft gradient that matches the participant’s accent color (derived from their avatar or a default palette). The gradient gently shifts for that brief connection window, then seamlessly fades into the live video feed.

That’s it. A colored gradient that lasts less than half a second.

Why it mattered

The difference was immediate. In user testing, people stopped perceiving a “loading” state at all. The transition from “joining” to “connected” felt continuous instead of stepped.

One developer testing the SDK said: “It just feels… alive. Even before the video starts.”

That word — alive — stuck with me. That’s exactly what a real-time video experience should feel like.

The lesson

Developer tools don’t get a pass on feel. Just because your users are engineers doesn’t mean they don’t notice when something feels rough or when something feels right.

The technical specs matter. Latency matters. Reliability matters. But the moments between the specs — the micro-interactions, the transitions, the defaults — those are what make a product feel like someone cared.

Building with intention

I think about this a lot now when making decisions for VibeLive. For every feature, I ask: what does the default experience feel like? Not the configured, optimized, fully-customized version — the out-of-the-box version.

Because that first impression, those first 400 milliseconds, that’s where trust is built.

The best developer tools don’t just work. They feel like they were made by someone who uses them.
